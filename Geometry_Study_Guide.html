Chapter 1

Foundations of Geometry

Undefined terms, defined terms, measurement, and the logic of proofs

What is Euclidean Geometry?

Euclidean geometry is the study of flat surfaces and the figures drawn on them. It was systematized by the ancient Greek mathematician Euclid around 300 BCE in his famous work Elements. Everything in this branch of geometry is built on a small set of rules called postulates, from which all other facts — called theorems — are logically derived.

Key Concept

Postulate: A statement accepted as true without proof.
Theorem: A statement that has been proven using postulates, definitions, and previously proven theorems.

Think of it this way: Postulates are the "rules of the game." Theorems are things you can prove by following those rules carefully.
Undefined and Defined Terms

Geometry starts with a small set of terms that are not formally defined — they are understood through intuition. From these, we define all other geometric objects.

Undefined Terms (The Big Three)

Point

An exact location in space. It has no size, no length, no width. Represented by a dot and named with a capital letter. Example: Point A

Line

A perfectly straight path that extends infinitely in both directions. It has no thickness. Named by two points on it or a lowercase letter. Example: Line AB or line ℓ

Plane

A flat, two-dimensional surface that extends infinitely in all directions. Think of an infinite sheet of paper. Named by three non-collinear points or a script letter. Example: Plane ABC or Plane P

Defined Terms

  • Line Segment (AB): Part of a line with two endpoints. It has a measurable length.
  • Ray (→AB): Starts at one point (endpoint) and extends infinitely in one direction.
  • Angle (∠): Formed by two rays that share a common endpoint called the vertex.
  • Collinear points: Points that all lie on the same line.
  • Coplanar points: Points that all lie on the same plane.
  • Midpoint: The point that divides a segment into two equal parts.
  • Bisector: A ray, line, or segment that cuts another figure into two equal parts.
Measuring Length and Angles

Measuring Distance Between Two Points

When two points are placed on a coordinate plane, you can find the exact distance between them using the Distance Formula, which is derived from the Pythagorean Theorem.

Distance Formula
d = √[(x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)²]
Worked Example

Find the distance between A(0, 0) and B(3, 4).

  1. Identify coordinates: x₁ = 0, y₁ = 0, x₂ = 3, y₂ = 4
  2. Plug into formula: d = √[(3−0)² + (4−0)²]
  3. Simplify: d = √[9 + 16] = √25
  4. Answer: d = 5 units

Measuring Angles

Angles are measured in degrees (°) using a protractor. There are four main types:

0° < θ < 90°
Acute Angle
θ = 90°
Right Angle
90° < θ < 180°
Obtuse Angle
θ = 180°
Straight Angle
Also important: A reflex angle is greater than 180° but less than 360°. A full rotation is exactly 360°.
Midpoint Formula

The midpoint M between two points A(x₁, y₁) and B(x₂, y₂) is:

M = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2)

Example: Midpoint of A(2, 4) and B(6, 10) → M = (4, 7)

Introduction to Proofs

A proof is a formal logical argument that shows a mathematical statement is true. Every step in a proof must be justified by a reason: a definition, postulate, or theorem.

Types of Proofs

  • Two-Column Proof: Statements in the left column, reasons in the right column. Most common in high school geometry.
  • Paragraph Proof: Written as sentences explaining the logical flow.
  • Flowchart Proof: Uses boxes and arrows to show the logical connections.

Common Proof Reasons

  • Given — information stated as true at the start
  • Definition of ___ — using the meaning of a term
  • Reflexive Property — a = a (any segment/angle equals itself)
  • Symmetric Property — if a = b, then b = a
  • Transitive Property — if a = b and b = c, then a = c
  • Substitution — replacing one equal expression with another
Sample Two-Column Proof

Prove: If ∠1 and ∠2 are supplementary and ∠2 = 50°, then ∠1 = 130°.

Statements Reasons
1. ∠1 and ∠2 are supplementary Given
2. ∠1 + ∠2 = 180° Definition of supplementary angles
3. ∠2 = 50° Given
4. ∠1 + 50° = 180° Substitution (step 3 into step 2)
5. ∠1 = 130° Subtraction Property of Equality

Angle Relationships

Linear pairs, vertical angles, complementary and supplementary relationships

Linear Pairs and Vertical Angles
Linear Pair

Two adjacent angles that together form a straight line (180°). They share a common vertex and a common ray, and their non-shared rays form a straight line. The two angles are always supplementary.

Vertical Angles

When two lines intersect, they form two pairs of opposite angles called vertical angles. Vertical angles are always congruent (equal in measure).

Visual Setup

When lines AB and CD intersect at point E, four angles are formed:
• ∠AEC and ∠BED are vertical angles → ∠AEC = ∠BED
• ∠AED and ∠BEC are vertical angles → ∠AED = ∠BEC
• ∠AEC and ∠AED are a linear pair → ∠AEC + ∠AED = 180°

Worked Example

Two lines intersect. One angle measures 65°. Find the other three angles.

  1. The vertical angle is also 65° (vertical angles are equal).
  2. The linear pair partner = 180° − 65° = 115°.
  3. The fourth angle (vertical to the 115°) = 115°.
  4. So the four angles are: 65°, 115°, 65°, 115°.
Complementary and Supplementary Angles
Complementary Angles

Two angles whose measures add up to 90°. They do not have to be adjacent — they just need to sum to 90°.

Supplementary Angles

Two angles whose measures add up to 180°. Again, they do not need to be adjacent to be supplementary.

Quick Reference
Complementary: A + B = 90°    Supplementary: A + B = 180°
Worked Examples

Example 1: Find the complement of 37°.
Complement = 90° − 37° = 53°

Example 2: Find the supplement of 112°.
Supplement = 180° − 112° = 68°

Example 3 (Algebra): Two complementary angles have measures (2x + 10)° and (x − 4)°. Find x.
(2x + 10) + (x − 4) = 90
3x + 6 = 90
3x = 84
x = 28

Memory trick: Complementary = Corner (right angle, 90°). Supplementary = Straight line (180°).

Transformations

Moving and changing figures using reflections, translations, rotations, and dilations

Overview of Transformations

A transformation is a rule that moves or changes every point of a figure in the coordinate plane. The original figure is called the pre-image, and the result is the image.

Translation

(x, y) → (x+a, y+b)

Slides the figure without flipping or rotating. Preserves size and shape.

Reflection

(x, y) → (−x, y) or (x, −y)

Flips the figure over a line of reflection. Preserves size and shape.

Rotation

(x, y) → (−y, x) for 90°

Turns the figure around a fixed point. Preserves size and shape.

Dilation

(x, y) → (kx, ky)

Scales the figure by a factor k. Preserves shape only — NOT size.

Key Distinction

Translations, reflections, and rotations are called rigid motions (isometries) — they preserve both size and shape, keeping figures congruent. Dilations are not rigid motions because they change size, creating similar (not congruent) figures.

Reflections

A reflection flips a figure over a line called the line of reflection. Every point in the image is the same distance from the line of reflection as the corresponding point in the pre-image, but on the opposite side.

Coordinate Rules for Common Reflections

Line of Reflection Rule Example
x-axis (x, y) → (x, −y) (3, 5) → (3, −5)
y-axis (x, y) → (−x, y) (3, 5) → (−3, 5)
y = x (x, y) → (y, x) (3, 5) → (5, 3)
y = −x (x, y) → (−y, −x) (3, 5) → (−5, −3)
Origin (x, y) → (−x, −y) (3, 5) → (−3, −5)
Worked Example

Reflect triangle with vertices A(1,2), B(3,4), C(5,1) across the y-axis.

Apply rule (x, y) → (−x, y):
A(1, 2) → A'(−1, 2)
B(3, 4) → B'(−3, 4)
C(5, 1) → C'(−5, 1)

Important: A reflection across the x-axis negates the y. A reflection across the y-axis negates the x. Don't mix them up!
Translations
Translation

A transformation that moves every point of a figure the same distance in the same direction. It is described by a translation vector <a, b>, where a is horizontal movement and b is vertical movement.

Translation Rule
(x, y) → (x + a, y + b)
Worked Example

Translate point P(2, −3) by vector <4, 6>.
New x = 2 + 4 = 6
New y = −3 + 6 = 3
Image point P'(6, 3)

Translate triangle A(0,0), B(2,0), C(1,3) by <−3, 2>.
A' = (0−3, 0+2) = (−3, 2)
B' = (2−3, 0+2) = (−1, 2)
C' = (1−3, 3+2) = (−2, 5)

Rotations

A rotation turns a figure around a fixed point called the center of rotation. The amount of turn is the angle of rotation, measured in degrees. Rotations are usually counterclockwise (positive) unless stated otherwise.

Standard Rotation Rules (about the Origin)

Rotation Rule Example: P(3, 5)
90° counterclockwise (x, y) → (−y, x) (3, 5) → (−5, 3)
180° (x, y) → (−x, −y) (3, 5) → (−3, −5)
270° counterclockwise (x, y) → (y, −x) (3, 5) → (5, −3)
360° (full rotation) (x, y) → (x, y) (3, 5) → (3, 5)
Trick: For 90° CCW: negate x, then swap. For 180°: negate both. For 270° CCW (same as 90° CW): negate y, then swap.
Worked Example

Rotate A(4, −2) by 90° counterclockwise about the origin.
Rule: (x, y) → (−y, x)
A(4, −2) → A'(−(−2), 4) = A'(2, 4)

Compositions of Transformations
Composition

A composition is when you apply two or more transformations in sequence. The result of the first transformation becomes the input for the second.

Order matters! A reflection followed by a translation gives a different result than a translation followed by a reflection. Always apply transformations from right to left (or as listed, left to right depending on notation).
Worked Example

Reflect P(2, 3) across the x-axis, then translate by <1, 4>.

  1. Reflect across x-axis: (2, 3) → (2, −3)
  2. Translate by <1, 4>: (2+1, −3+4) = (3, 1)
Symmetry
Line (Reflectional) Symmetry

A figure has line symmetry if it can be folded along a line so that both halves match exactly. The fold line is the line of symmetry.

Rotational Symmetry

A figure has rotational symmetry if it looks the same after being rotated by some angle less than 360°. The minimum angle that maps the figure to itself is the angle of rotational symmetry.

Figure Lines of Symmetry Rotational Symmetry
Equilateral Triangle 3 120°, 240°, 360°
Square 4 90°, 180°, 270°, 360°
Regular Pentagon 5 72°, 144°, 216°, 288°, 360°
Rectangle (non-square) 2 180°, 360°
Circle ∞ Any angle

Lines and Their Relationships

Parallel lines, perpendicular lines, transversals, and writing equations

Parallel and Perpendicular Lines
Parallel Lines

Lines in the same plane that never intersect. They have the same slope. Symbol: ∥
Example: y = 3x + 1 and y = 3x − 5 are parallel.

Perpendicular Lines

Lines that intersect at exactly 90°. Their slopes are negative reciprocals of each other. Symbol: ⊥
Example: slopes 2 and −½ are perpendicular.

Slope Relationships
Parallel: m₁ = m₂      Perpendicular: m₁ × m₂ = −1
Worked Example

Line ℓ has slope 4. What is the slope of a line perpendicular to ℓ?
Perpendicular slope = −1/4 (flip the fraction, change the sign).

Are y = −2x + 7 and y = −2x − 3 parallel?
Both have slope −2. Yes, they are parallel (different y-intercepts confirm they don't overlap).

Lines Cut by a Transversal

A transversal is a line that crosses two or more other lines. When it crosses two parallel lines, it creates 8 angles with special relationships.

Angle Pair Definitions

Corresponding Angles: Same position at each intersection. If lines are parallel, they are congruent.
Alternate Interior Angles: On opposite sides of the transversal, between the parallel lines. If lines are parallel, they are congruent.
Alternate Exterior Angles: On opposite sides of the transversal, outside the parallel lines. If lines are parallel, they are congruent.
Co-Interior (Same-Side Interior) Angles: On the same side of the transversal, between the lines. If lines are parallel, they are supplementary (sum = 180°).

Angle Pair Location If lines are ∥...
Corresponding Same position at each crossing Congruent (equal)
Alternate Interior Opposite sides, inside Congruent (equal)
Alternate Exterior Opposite sides, outside Congruent (equal)
Co-Interior (Same-Side Interior) Same side, inside Supplementary (180°)
Worked Example

Two parallel lines are cut by a transversal. One co-interior angle is 72°. Find its co-interior partner.
Co-interior angles are supplementary: 180° − 72° = 108°

Proving Lines Parallel

To prove two lines are parallel, show that one of the following conditions holds (these are the converses of the transversal theorems):

  • Corresponding angles are congruent → lines are parallel
  • Alternate interior angles are congruent → lines are parallel
  • Alternate exterior angles are congruent → lines are parallel
  • Co-interior angles are supplementary → lines are parallel
Worked Example

Two lines are cut by a transversal. Alternate interior angles measure 58° and 58°. Are the lines parallel?
Yes. Because the alternate interior angles are congruent, by the Converse of the Alternate Interior Angles Theorem, the lines are parallel.

Writing Linear Equations

Forms of Linear Equations

Slope-Intercept Form

y = mx + b
m = slope, b = y-intercept
Best for: graphing

Point-Slope Form

y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)
Use when you know a point and a slope
Best for: writing equations

Worked Examples

Example 1: Write an equation through (2, 5) with slope 3.
y − 5 = 3(x − 2) → y = 3x − 1

Example 2: Write an equation through (1, 4) parallel to y = 2x + 7.
Parallel → same slope m = 2.
y − 4 = 2(x − 1) → y = 2x + 2

Example 3: Write an equation through (3, −1) perpendicular to y = 3x + 5.
Perpendicular slope = −1/3.
y − (−1) = −1/3(x − 3) → y = −x/3 + 0 → y = −x/3

Triangles

Angle theorems, classification, inequalities, isosceles properties, and triangle centers

Triangle Angle Theorems
Triangle Angle Sum Theorem

The sum of the three interior angles of any triangle is always 180°. This is true for ALL triangles regardless of shape or size.

Triangle Angle Sum
∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180°
Exterior Angle Theorem

An exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent (remote) interior angles.

Exterior Angle Theorem
∠exterior = ∠A + ∠B (where A, B are the two remote interior angles)
Worked Examples

Example 1: A triangle has angles 55° and 80°. Find the third angle.
Third angle = 180° − 55° − 80° = 45°

Example 2: An exterior angle of a triangle is 120°. One remote interior angle is 70°. Find the other.
Other remote interior angle = 120° − 70° = 50°

Example 3 (Algebra): Angles are (x+10)°, (2x)°, and (x−6)°. Find x.
(x+10) + 2x + (x−6) = 180
4x + 4 = 180 → x = 44

Classifying Triangles

By Angle Measure

  • Acute Triangle: All three angles are less than 90°
  • Right Triangle: One angle is exactly 90°
  • Obtuse Triangle: One angle is greater than 90°
  • Equiangular: All three angles equal 60° (same as equilateral)
Triangle Inequality Theorems
Triangle Inequality Theorem

For any triangle, the sum of the lengths of any two sides must be greater than the length of the third side. If this fails for any combination, the segments cannot form a triangle.

Must All Be True
a + b > c     a + c > b     b + c > a
Worked Examples

Can 5, 8, 11 form a triangle?
5 + 8 = 13 > 11 ✓
5 + 11 = 16 > 8 ✓
8 + 11 = 19 > 5 ✓
→ Yes, these form a valid triangle.

Can 2, 3, 7 form a triangle?
2 + 3 = 5, but 5 is NOT > 7. ✗
→ No, these cannot form a triangle.

Finding the range of a third side: Given sides 6 and 10, what values can the third side x have?
|10 − 6| < x < 10 + 6
4 < x < 16

Isosceles Triangle Theorem
Isosceles Triangle Theorem

If two sides of a triangle are congruent (equal in length), then the angles opposite those sides are also congruent (equal in measure). These equal angles are called base angles.

Converse

If two angles of a triangle are congruent, then the sides opposite those angles are also congruent.

Worked Example

In isosceles triangle ABC, AB = AC. The vertex angle ∠A = 40°. Find the base angles.
Base angles are equal. Let each = x.
40 + x + x = 180
2x = 140
x = 70°
So ∠B = ∠C = 70°.

If ∠B = ∠C = 55° in isosceles △ABC, find ∠A.
∠A = 180 − 55 − 55 = 70°

Special case: An equilateral triangle is a special isosceles triangle where all three sides AND all three angles are equal (each angle = 60°).
Triangle Centers

A triangle has four main "special points" inside (or on) it, each defined by a different set of construction lines.

Centroid (G)

Where the three medians intersect. A median connects a vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side. The centroid is always inside the triangle and is the triangle's center of gravity (balance point).

Key fact: The centroid divides each median in a 2:1 ratio from vertex to midpoint.

Orthocenter (H)

Where the three altitudes intersect. An altitude is a perpendicular segment from a vertex to the opposite side (or its extension).

Key fact: The orthocenter can be inside (acute triangle), on (right triangle), or outside (obtuse triangle) the triangle.

Incenter (I)

Where the three angle bisectors intersect. It is the center of the inscribed circle (incircle) — the largest circle that fits inside the triangle.

Key fact: The incenter is always inside the triangle.

Circumcenter (C)

Where the three perpendicular bisectors of the sides intersect. It is the center of the circumscribed circle (circumcircle) — the circle that passes through all three vertices.

Key fact: Can be inside, on, or outside the triangle depending on type.

Memory trick: "Altitudes → ortHocenter. Medians → centroid. Angle bisectors → Incenter. PerPendicular bisectors → circuMcenter."

Triangle Congruence

Proving triangles identical using side and angle relationships

Congruent Figures
Congruent Figures

Two figures are congruent (≅) if they have exactly the same shape and size. All corresponding sides are equal in length and all corresponding angles are equal in measure.

CPCTC

Corresponding Parts of Congruent Triangles are Congruent. Once you prove two triangles are congruent, you can conclude that any corresponding parts are also congruent. This is frequently used as a reason in proofs.

Triangle Congruence Theorems

You do not need to show all six pairs of equal parts (3 sides + 3 angles) to prove triangles congruent. The following shortcuts work:

SSS
Three pairs of corresponding sides are equal
SAS
Two sides and the included angle between them are equal
ASA
Two angles and the included side between them are equal
AAS
Two angles and a non-included side are equal
HL
Right triangles only: Hypotenuse and one Leg are equal
✗ SSA
NOT a valid congruence theorem (ambiguous case)
Important! SSA (two sides and a non-included angle) does NOT guarantee congruence — there may be two possible triangles. The only exception is HL for right triangles.
Identifying the Right Theorem

Which theorem applies?

  • AB = DE, BC = EF, AC = DF → SSS
  • ∠A = ∠D, AB = DE, ∠B = ∠E → ASA (angle-side-angle with included side AB/DE)
  • ∠A = ∠D, ∠B = ∠E, BC = EF (non-included) → AAS
  • Right triangles, hypotenuse AB = DE, leg AC = DF → HL
Two-Column Proof Using SAS

Given: M is the midpoint of both diagonals. Prove: △ABM ≅ △CDM

Statements Reasons
1. M is the midpoint of both diagonals Given
2. AM = CM and BM = DM Definition of midpoint
3. ∠AMB = ∠CMD Vertical angles are congruent
4. △ABM ≅ △CDM SAS (steps 2, 3, 2)

Dilations and Similarity

Scaling figures and proving triangles similar using AA, SSS, and SAS

Dilations
Dilation

A dilation is a transformation that produces a figure similar to the original by scaling all distances from a fixed point called the center of dilation by a number called the scale factor (k).

Dilation Rule (centered at origin)
(x, y) → (kx, ky)
  • If k > 1: the image is an enlargement (larger than original)
  • If 0 < k < 1: the image is a reduction (smaller than original)
  • If k = 1: the image is congruent to the original
  • If k < 0: the image is on the opposite side of the center
Worked Example

Dilate triangle A(2,4), B(6,4), C(4,8) by scale factor k = 1/2 centered at origin.
A' = (1, 2)   B' = (3, 2)   C' = (2, 4)

Dilate point P(−3, 5) by k = 3.
P' = (3×−3, 3×5) = (−9, 15)

Similar Figures
Similar Figures (~)

Two figures are similar if they have the same shape but not necessarily the same size. In similar figures, corresponding angles are equal and corresponding sides are proportional.

Proportion of Similar Figures
AB/DE = BC/EF = AC/DF = k (scale factor)
Worked Example

△ABC ~ △DEF with AB = 6, BC = 9, DE = 4. Find EF.
Set up proportion: AB/DE = BC/EF
6/4 = 9/EF
6 × EF = 4 × 9 = 36
EF = 6

Triangle Similarity Theorems
AA
Two pairs of corresponding angles are equal. (Third angle must also match since all sum to 180°)
SSS~
All three pairs of sides are in the same proportion (ratio)
SAS~
Two sides are proportional AND the included angle between them is equal
AA is the most commonly used similarity theorem. If you can show two angles of one triangle equal two angles of another, the triangles are automatically similar.
Worked Examples

Example 1 (AA): △ABC has ∠A = 50°, ∠B = 70°. △DEF has ∠D = 50°, ∠E = 70°. Are they similar?
Yes! Two pairs of angles match → AA Similarity → △ABC ~ △DEF

Example 2 (SSS~): △ABC has sides 4, 6, 8. △DEF has sides 6, 9, 12. Are they similar?
4/6 = 2/3, 6/9 = 2/3, 8/12 = 2/3. All ratios equal → SSS Similarity → △ABC ~ △DEF

Right Triangle Similarity
Geometric Mean (Altitude) Theorem

When an altitude is drawn from the right angle of a right triangle to the hypotenuse, it creates three similar triangles: the original and the two smaller ones. This relationship leads to the geometric mean.

Key Relationships
altitude² = (left hyp. segment) × (right hyp. segment)
Worked Example

In right triangle ABC, altitude CD is drawn to hypotenuse AB. AD = 4 and DB = 9. Find CD.
CD² = AD × DB = 4 × 9 = 36
CD = √36 = 6

Directed Line Segments

A directed line segment is a segment with a specific direction, from point A to point B. Unlike a regular segment (which has no direction), directed line segments are used in coordinate geometry to model movement and partition segments.

Partitioning a Segment

To divide segment AB so that the point P is at ratio m:n from A to B:

Partition Formula
P = A + [m/(m+n)] × (B − A)
Worked Example

Find the point that divides A(1, 2) to B(7, 8) in a ratio of 1:2.
m = 1, n = 2 → m/(m+n) = 1/3
x = 1 + (1/3)(7−1) = 1 + 2 = 3
y = 2 + (1/3)(8−2) = 2 + 2 = 4
Point P = (3, 4)
